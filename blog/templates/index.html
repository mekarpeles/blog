OpenJournal

Mek, October 22, 2012

Alpha Launch

I'm proud to announce the α-launch of OpenJournal - A community for sharing, discussing, and curating the world's best computer science, academic papers. OpenJournal is a non-proffit service which aims to help progress the field of computer science by (1) making CS research accessible to the public, faster and (2) providing an interface which facilitates discovery of new relevant, novel, and impactful papers. This is accomplished, in part, by enabling our community to rank/vote and commment on papers.

What's next?

Our vision doesn't just apply to information collection and retrieval. Over time, it is our intention to influence, reform, and improve the actual academic journal-writing process in three ways: (1) to increase the overall quality of papers, (2) to increase distributed, collaborative efforts around the world, and (3) to increase the rate at which papers are co-developed and improved. These objectives require attacking key aspects like making papers implementable, testable, forkable (like git forking), searchable, annotatable and most importantly.

OpenJournal is brought to you by the Lambda Herders; a team of computer scientists and entrepreneurs from the San Francisco Bay area. We are in the process of coordinating partnerships and sponsorships with several of the nationals top CS schools and research facilities. Please contact us if you are interested in getting involved. Special thanks to Yifan Peng, Peter L Downs, Zephyr Pellerin, Stephen Balaban, Chris Tucci, Dan Cote.

LazyDB

Mek, September 19, 2012

LazyDB is a basic wrapper around the Python shelve flatfile dbm module. LazyDB provides a couchdb/S3 like layer of abstraction around shelve, allowing convenient access (read and write) to shelve without sacrificing the convenience of writing pickled python objects to disk.

From the help(shelve) documentation: A "shelf" is a persistent, dictionary-like object. The difference with dbm databases is that the values (not the keys!) in a shelf can be essentially arbitrary Python objects -- anything that the "pickle" module can handle. This includes most class instances, recursive data types, and objects containing lots of shared sub-objects. The keys are ordinary strings.

Disclaimer: I wouldn't suggest using this module for production projects as I predict it having difficulty scaling. LazyDB is ideal for supporting smaller projects with light data in which the programmer must move quickly and intends to implement an alternate db solution sooner rather than later. The intent is to provide a more elegant interface for utilizing the shelve module, not to create an efficient replacement.

A Greyhat's Doctrine

Mek, September 18, 2012

Enthusiasm is a powerful feeling. It can motivate us to act impulsively, to overcome shyness, and drive us insistently to persevere. However, enthusiasm, like a drug, comes in different doses and has side effects; both good and bad. Enthusiasm can blur one's vision, causing the boundaries which define our principles to bend, bleed, and distort. On one hand, it may give us the courage to step outside our comfort level, to experiment with / find exceptions (or deviations) within our belief system. However, it can also drive us to lose perspective, become desensitized, and impair our ability to think objectively.

Like symbolic logic systems, our thought process is governed by a set of axioms. These axioms represent our fundamental beliefs of how the universe works and are derived from our knowledge base of experiences and learnings. We hold these axiomatic beliefs to be self-evident in that we have tested and tried their soundness to an extend which adequately satisfies our needs. Such an axiom may be humans require oxygen to sustain life. From these axioms, these truths, we are able make decisions; to establish and evolve principles, morals, and ethics which we use to govern and justify our actions.

When we leverage enthusiasm to attack a problem, we are acting under a personal bias which doesn't necessarily follow from our axioms. It's like eating chocolate cake because we enjoy it, even if doing so is detremental to our heath. Such behaviour can cause our knowledge bases to be poluted (in the form of the mutation or introduction of axioms). An enthusiastic video game player may be swayed to evolve an axiomatic understanding that gaming is intrinsicly a good use of time, for instance.

That isn't to say enthusiasm is a bad thing. It is rather, a form of stochasticism which allows us to be unique individuals, resulting in the exploration of topics which interest us. However, like any scientific experiment, our goal when modifying our principle system should be to understand each modification to our system, as well as the implications that come with it. When too many changes occur at once, it becomes challenging to verify and affirm our existing axioms. As a result, I believe it perimount that one learn to control their enthusiasm and to seek a balance, as to maximize its benefits without jeopardizing one's principles.

One of my life goals is to strive to live the life of a white hat. I ground myself by the following doctrine:

Above all, hack for the great benefit of others.
Struggle not to obtain freedoms from your oppressor,
hack to create freedoms for the oppressed.
Pay for freedom with righteous hacks, not in blood.
Hack abstractions such that mutually exclusive ideologies may peacefully coincide.
Hack the present into obsolescence.
Hack the past towards the future.
Hack together, not each other

And don't be afraid to get your hat a little dirty.
